## EVALUATION LINEAR MODEL: FOOD_SHARE ~ INCOME
The estimated coefficient on quarterly income is −1.461 × 10⁻⁶, indicating a negative relationship whereby higher income is associated with a lower food expenditure share, which is consistent with Engel's Law. The coefficient is precisely estimated, with a standard error of 6.15 × 10⁻⁸, resulting in a large t-statistic of −23.761 and a p-value of 0.000, which strongly rejects the null hypothesis of no relationship at any conventional significance level.
However, the R-squared value of 0.038 suggests that income alone explains only about 3.8% of the variation in food share, indicating that while the relationship is statistically significant, the linear model has limited explanatory power. Furthermore, the high condition number (4.99 × 10⁴) warns of potential numerical instability, and the significant Omnibus and Jarque-Bera tests (p = 0.000) indicate that the residuals are not normally distributed, with notable positive skewness (1.211) and excess kurtosis (3.452), which may violate OLS assumptions.

## EVALUATION SEMI-LOG MODEL: FOOD_SHARE ~ LOG_INCOME
The estimated coefficient on log quarterly income is −0.0324, indicating that a 1% increase in income is associated with a decrease of approximately 0.000324 in food expenditure share, on average. This negative relationship is consistent with Engel's Law. The coefficient is precisely estimated with a standard error of 0.001, yielding a t-statistic of −24.565 and a p-value of 0.000, strongly rejecting the null hypothesis that income has no effect on food expenditure share.
Compared to the linear model, the semi-log specification performs slightly better, with a marginally higher R-squared of 0.041 and a lower AIC (−5967 vs −5930) and BIC (−5952 vs −5915), suggesting a better model fit. The condition number also drops significantly to 75.6, resolving the numerical instability concern seen in the linear model. Nevertheless, the residuals still show non-normality based on the Jarque-Bera test, implying that caution should be taken when making inference based solely on OLS standard errors.

## MODEL COMPARISON
Comparing the two specifications, the semi-log model (FOOD_SHARE ~ LOG_INCOME) is preferred over the linear model (FOOD_SHARE ~ INCOME) for several reasons.
First, in terms of goodness of fit, the semi-log model achieves a slightly higher R-squared (0.041 vs 0.038) and lower information criteria values (AIC: −5967 vs −5930; BIC: −5952 vs −5915), indicating a better overall fit to the data.
Second, the semi-log model is theoretically more appropriate for capturing the relationship described by Engel's Law. Since income tends to be right-skewed and its effect on food share is likely to diminish at higher income levels, applying a log transformation to income better reflects this non-linear, diminishing relationship compared to assuming a purely linear one.
Third, the condition number in the semi-log model (75.6) is substantially lower than that of the linear model (4.99 × 10⁴), suggesting that the log transformation effectively reduces numerical instability and produces more reliable coefficient estimates.
Overall, while neither model explains a large proportion of the variance in food expenditure share — suggesting that other factors beyond income also play a role — the semi-log model provides a more theoretically grounded and statistically reliable framework for testing Engel's Law. Future work could consider incorporating additional variables such as household size, geographic region, or demographic characteristics to improve explanatory power.
